📚 What is Calculus?

Calculus is the mathematical study of continuous change. It has two major branches:

📈 Differential Calculus

Concerned with rates of change and slopes of curves

📊 Integral Calculus

Concerned with accumulation of quantities and areas under curves

💡 Real-Life Context

Think of calculus as the mathematics of motion and change. When you drive a car, your speedometer shows your instantaneous rate of change (derivative), while your odometer shows the total distance traveled (integral).

🎯 Limits: The Foundation

What is a Limit?

A limit describes the value that a function approaches as the input approaches some value.

Notation: limx → a f(x) = L

"The limit of f(x) as x approaches a equals L"

Simple Example

Consider f(x) = (x² - 1)/(x - 1)

As x approaches 1, f(x) approaches 2

🚗 Real-Life Example: Approaching a Stop Sign

Imagine you're driving toward a stop sign. As you get closer and closer to the sign (but haven't reached it yet), your distance to the sign approaches zero.

  • At 100 feet away → you're far
  • At 10 feet away → you're close
  • At 1 foot away → you're very close
  • Limit: As you approach the sign, your distance approaches 0

This is exactly what a limit represents – what value we're getting closer to, even if we never actually reach it!

📈 Derivatives: Rates of Change

What is a Derivative?

A derivative measures how a function changes as its input changes. It represents the instantaneous rate of change or the slope of the tangent line.

Notation: f'(x) or dy/dx

Definition: f'(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) - f(x)]/h

Basic Rules

Function Derivative
f(x) = c (constant) f'(x) = 0
f(x) = xⁿ f'(x) = nxⁿ⁻¹
f(x) = eˣ f'(x) = eˣ
f(x) = sin(x) f'(x) = cos(x)

🚀 Real-Life Example: Car Speed and Acceleration

Your position while driving can be described by a function s(t), where t is time.

  • Position: s(t) = where you are
  • Velocity (1st derivative): v(t) = s'(t) = how fast you're going
  • Acceleration (2nd derivative): a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) = how quickly your speed is changing

Scenario:

If your position is s(t) = t² (in meters, t in seconds):

  • Velocity: v(t) = s'(t) = 2t m/s
  • Acceleration: a(t) = v'(t) = 2 m/s²

At t = 5 seconds:

  • You've traveled 25 meters
  • You're moving at 10 m/s
  • Your acceleration is constant at 2 m/s²

💰 Another Example: Business Profit

If P(x) represents profit from selling x items:

  • P'(x) tells you the marginal profit - how much extra profit you make from selling one more item
  • When P'(x) = 0, you've found maximum profit!

✏️ Practice Problems

  1. Find the derivative of f(x) = x³
    2. Solution

    f'(x) = 3x²

  2. Find the derivative of f(x) = 5x² + 3x
    3. Solution

    f'(x) = 10x + 3

📊 Integrals: Accumulation

What is an Integral?

An integral represents the accumulation of quantities or the area under a curve. It's the reverse operation of differentiation.

Indefinite Integral: ∫ f(x)dx = F(x) + C

Definite Integral: ∫ab f(x)dx = F(b) - F(a)

Key Concept: The Fundamental Theorem

Differentiation and integration are inverse operations!

  • If you differentiate then integrate, you get back to where you started
  • If you integrate then differentiate, you also get back to where you started

💧 Real-Life Example: Filling a Water Tank

Imagine water flowing into a tank at a rate of r(t) liters per minute.

  • r(t) = flow rate (liters/minute)
  • ∫ r(t)dt = total amount of water accumulated

Scenario:

If water flows at r(t) = 2t liters/minute:

  • After 5 minutes, total water = ∫₀⁵ 2t dt = [t²]₀⁵ = 25 liters
  • The integral accumulates all the water that flowed over time

🚗 Another Example: Distance from Speed

If you know your velocity v(t) at every moment:

  • Total Distance = ∫ v(t)dt
  • The integral adds up all the tiny distances traveled in each instant

This is why your car's odometer works - it integrates your speed over time!

💵 Business Application: Total Revenue

If marginal revenue MR(x) tells you revenue from selling one more item:

  • Total Revenue = ∫ MR(x)dx
  • Integrate marginal revenue to get total revenue

✏️ Practice Problems

  1. Find ∫ x² dx
    2. Solution

    ∫ x² dx = x³/3 + C

  2. Calculate ∫₀² 3x² dx
    3. Solution

    [x³]₀² = 8 - 0 = 8

🎯 Detailed Example: Optimizing a Garden

You have 100 meters of fencing and want to create a rectangular garden with maximum area.

Solution Using Calculus:

  1. Let width = w, length = l
  2. Perimeter: 2w + 2l = 100 → l = 50 - w
  3. Area: A(w) = w × l = w(50 - w) = 50w - w²
  4. Find maximum: A'(w) = 50 - 2w
  5. Set A'(w) = 0: 50 - 2w = 0 → w = 25
  6. Therefore: l = 25

Result: A square garden (25m × 25m) gives maximum area of 625 m²!
